# Editorial Standards — The Freethinking Times

This document is the editorial constitution of The Freethinking Times. It applies to
all contributors — staff writers, freelancers, guest columnists, and editors. It covers
what we publish, how we report it, and what we owe our readers.

---

## Mission and Editorial Philosophy

The Freethinking Times exists to do the journalism and intellectual work that the
institutional press increasingly cannot or will not do: long-form investigation of how
power actually operates, philosophy taken seriously rather than performed, and ideas
argued rather than signaled.

**Investigative** means we follow the evidence wherever it leads — into regulatory
agencies, grant structures, corporate governance, and the administrative decisions made
quietly in rooms where no journalist was invited. We are not interested in access. We
do not soften findings to preserve relationships.

**Philosophical** means we treat ideas as consequential. We believe that how people
reason matters — that epistemology, ethics, and the philosophy of science have real
consequences for real people — and that the freethinking tradition from Paine and Hume
through Russell and Ingersoll offers tools that contemporary intellectual life has
largely abandoned.

**Adversarial to power** means we do not mistake institutional authority for truth.
We are adversarial to power regardless of which faction of power holds it. We do not
have ideological commitments that precede the evidence; we have a commitment to the
evidence that precedes all ideology.

What we are not: we are not an aggregator, a hot-take mill, or an advocacy outlet.
We do not publish content whose purpose is to signal group membership. We do not
publish pieces that dress assertion as argument or advocacy as reporting.

## Voice and Tone

The publication voice is serious without being ponderous, direct without being glib,
and intellectually demanding without being academic. We write for a reader who is
intelligent and curious but not a specialist in the topic at hand.

**What to avoid:**

- **False balance** — presenting two positions as equally evidenced when one is
  better supported. "Some say X, others say Y" is not journalism; it is stenography.
- **The passive voice of institutional press releases** — "It was announced that,"
  "Officials said that steps are being taken to." Name the actor. State the action.
- **Hedging as a substitute for thinking** — epistemic humility is a virtue; performed
  epistemic humility is a way of avoiding a conclusion you should have reached.
- **Jargon** — write in English. If a concept requires a technical term, define it.
- **The five-Ws lede** — acceptable in news; deadening in everything else.
- **Throat-clearing intros** — "In today's complex world," "Now more than ever,"
  "Throughout human history." Start with the thing.

**Tonal differences by section:**

- **News** is plain and factual. Declarative sentences. Active verbs. The reporter
  is not present in the prose.
- **Opinion** is argued. The writer's voice and position are explicit. Evidence is
  marshalled; the reader should be able to follow the reasoning and disagree.
- **Analysis** is evidenced and discursive. It may be more speculative than news but
  must be clear about what is established fact and what is interpretation.
- **History** is narrative. It earns its relevance; it does not assert it.
- **Arts & Culture** may be more personal in register but must still make an argument,
  not merely express a preference.

## Sections — What Belongs Where

### News

**Covers:** Reported stories based on documents, data, interviews, and observation.
City hall, regulatory agencies, courts, corporate governance, public institutions.

**Belongs here:** Breaking news with dateline; investigative findings; primary-source
document reporting; reported features. Dateline is required. Byline is the reporter.

**Does not belong here:** Opinion dressed as reporting; analysis without a news hook;
commentary on other outlets' coverage.

**The distinguishing question:** Can every factual claim in this piece be sourced to
a document, a named source, or direct observation?

---

### Opinion

**Covers:** Signed argument. The author is making a case in their own voice.
Essays, columns, and editorial positions.

**Belongs here:** Arguments about policy, ethics, philosophy, or public affairs;
responses to other pieces; first-person intellectual essays; analysis that takes a
declared position.

**Does not belong here:** Reported facts presented as opinion; personal grievance
without general application; content that is purely emotive rather than argued.

**The distinguishing question:** Is the author making a claim they are willing to
defend with evidence and reasoning — not just asserting a position?

---

### Analysis

**Covers:** Evidence-based deep dives into systems, patterns, and mechanisms.
May include original data analysis. Longer form than news.

**Belongs here:** Data-driven investigation; policy analysis; research synthesis;
academic findings translated for a general audience; long-form investigative narrative.

**Does not belong here:** Advocacy; unsupported speculation; reporting that belongs
in News but has been labeled Analysis to avoid sourcing standards.

**The distinguishing question:** Does this piece show its work? Can a skeptical reader
follow the reasoning and identify where they disagree?

---

### Arts & Culture

**Covers:** Books, film, music, theatre, visual art, intellectual culture.
Reviews, profiles, and cultural criticism.

**Belongs here:** Reviews of specific works; profiles of artists and intellectuals;
criticism of cultural trends; essays about the relationship between ideas and their
cultural expression.

**Does not belong here:** Celebrity coverage without intellectual content; content
that is purely promotional; reviews that do not actually assess the work.

**The distinguishing question:** Does this piece have a specific subject and a
defensible argument about it?

---

### Science & Technology

**Covers:** Actual science. Findings, methods, replication, policy implications,
and the institutional structures that shape what gets studied and published.

**Belongs here:** Reported science journalism from primary sources (papers, data,
researchers); coverage of the replication crisis; regulatory science; technology
policy; the sociology of scientific institutions.

**Does not belong here:** Uncritical press release amplification; technology hype;
"scientists say" journalism without engaging the actual research; science-adjacent
speculation without appropriate epistemic labeling.

**The distinguishing question:** Does this piece engage with the actual evidence,
or does it take institutional claims at face value?

---

### History

**Covers:** Historical context, narrative, and analysis. Does not require a news hook
but should have a reason for publication now — a connection to current events,
a neglected story, or a correction of the historical record.

**Belongs here:** Historical narrative; biography; the history of ideas;
reassessment of historical consensus; primary-source history.

**Does not belong here:** Historical trivia without a point; revisionism without
evidence; nostalgia.

**The distinguishing question:** Why does this historical story matter to a reader
in the present moment?

---

### Letters

**Covers:** Reader correspondence responding to published pieces.

**Belongs here:** Responses to specific published articles; factual corrections
from readers with relevant expertise; substantive disagreements with published arguments.
Letters should be under 400 words.

**Does not belong here:** General commentary not responding to a specific piece;
extended essays (submit as Opinion instead); abuse.

**The distinguishing question:** Does this letter respond to a specific piece, and
does it add something a reader of that piece would benefit from reading?

---

### Reviews

**Covers:** Books, films, podcasts, documentaries, and other discrete works assessed
on their merits.

**Belongs here:** Honest assessments of specific works. The reviewer must have read
the book, watched the film, or listened to the podcast in full. Reviews must say
something beyond "this is good" or "this is bad."

**Does not belong here:** Coverage of works the reviewer has not completed;
promotional pieces; reviews that are really arguments about the topic rather than
assessments of the work.

**The distinguishing question:** Has the reviewer actually engaged with this work,
and can they tell a potential reader whether it is worth their time and why?

## Sourcing Standards

**Primary sources are required where available.** Documents, datasets, court filings,
regulatory records, and on-record interviews are primary sources. Use them. If a
primary source is available, citing a secondary source instead is not acceptable.

**Secondary sources** (other publications' reporting) are acceptable to corroborate
established facts and to cite findings you cannot independently verify. Only use
secondary sources with clear editorial standards — outlets that have bylines, editors,
and a corrections policy. Aggregators and anonymous blogs are not acceptable secondary
sources.

**Verification:** Any contested factual claim requires two independent sources.
One source is enough to note that a claim is made; it is not enough to present the
claim as established fact.

**Anonymous sources** are permitted only when all of the following are true:
1. The source has direct, first-hand knowledge of the facts.
2. The source cannot be identified without risk of significant harm (job loss, legal
   exposure, physical danger).
3. The information is corroborated by at least one other source or document.
4. The source's identity is known to the editor.

Anonymous sources may not be used for characterization of third parties, expressions
of opinion, or attacks on named individuals. "A source who asked not to be named called
the mayor 'corrupt'" is not acceptable.

**Artificial intelligence tools** may be used for research assistance — gathering
background, identifying potential sources, summarizing large documents. They may not be
used as source material. Every factual claim in a published piece must be traceable
to a primary or secondary source a reader could independently consult.

## Attribution and Quotes

**Direct quotes must be verbatim.** Do not reconstruct or composite quotes. If you
cannot quote accurately from notes or recording, paraphrase.

**Paraphrase must be labeled.** "According to Johnson," not "Johnson said," unless
what follows is a direct quote.

**"Said" vs. "according to":**
- Use `said` for direct quotes from interviews.
- Use `according to` for documents, reports, and written sources.
- Use `wrote` for articles, books, and correspondence.

**Pre-publication review:** We do not share full drafts with sources before publication.
We will read back direct quotes for accuracy confirmation. We will not allow sources
to approve, modify, or veto the surrounding text.

**Attribution for documents:** Name the document, its author or issuing body, and
its date where possible. "According to a 2024 audit by the City Controller's office"
is better than "according to documents reviewed by The Freethinking Times."

## Headlines and Subheadings

**Headlines** should use active verbs and make a specific claim. Avoid:

- False mystery ("You won't believe what happened next")
- Puns in news sections
- Vague superlatives ("A major new investigation")
- Questions that the article doesn't conclusively answer (Betteridge's Law applies)

Opinion and Analysis headlines may be more stylized, but they should still make
a claim rather than merely gesture at a topic.

**Subheadings** should function as a standalone outline of the piece. A reader
skimming subheadings should understand what the article argues and how. Write them
in sentence case, without a trailing period.

**The `description:` field** is both the SEO meta description and the card text
that appears across the site — in section listings, the homepage, RSS feeds,
and social shares. It must:
- Be complete sentences.
- Be ≤180 characters (150 is safer).
- Work without the headline for context.
- Not duplicate the headline word for word.
- Not start with "In this article" or "This piece argues."

## Lead Conventions

**News** leads use the inverted pyramid: most newsworthy fact first, then context.
The reader should understand what happened in the first two sentences.

**Opinion** leads may begin with the thesis, an anecdote, or a scene, but must
arrive at the central argument within two paragraphs. Do not hold your point
until the end.

**Analysis** leads may begin with a case study or data point that exemplifies
the pattern under examination. The pattern itself must be articulated within
the first 300 words.

**Avoid in all sections:**
- Throat-clearing ("In today's complex world…")
- Self-reference ("In this piece, I will argue…")
- Lede burial (writing three paragraphs before saying what the piece is about)
- The royal "we" for a single author

## Images

**Required fields** when using an image: both `image:` (path) and `imageAlt:` (alt
text) must be present. If you include one, you must include the other.

**Alt text standards:** Describe the image's content and context — not just its
visual appearance. Write for a reader who cannot see the image.

- Good: `"Protest sign reading 'Tax the Rich' held at a rally outside the Colorado state capitol, 2024"`
- Bad: `"Photo of protest"`

**Acceptable image sources:**
- Original photography (you hold the rights)
- Wikimedia Commons images with CC license or public domain designation
- Public domain works (confirm the specific license)
- Licensed stock photography (confirm the license covers publication)

Do not use watermarked images or images whose rights are unclear.

**Minimum dimensions:** 1200 × 630 pixels for Open Graph (social share) compatibility.
Images smaller than this will appear distorted or cropped in social previews.

## Corrections Policy

We do not issue stealth corrections. Every change to published content after
initial publication is logged.

**A correction is required when:**
- A factual claim is wrong.
- A person was misidentified or misquoted.
- A number, date, or name is wrong.
- The framing was materially misleading in a way that left readers with a false
  impression of the facts.

**A note (not a correction) is appropriate when:**
- Context that was not available at publication time is now available, but the
  original reporting was accurate with what was known.
- The situation has changed since publication, but the original was accurate.

**A retraction is required when:**
- The foundational premise of the article is false.
- The reporting was based on fabricated or knowingly false information.
- The piece cannot be corrected without being substantially rewritten to the point
  where the original no longer exists.

**How to log a correction:**

Append to the `corrections:` array in the article's frontmatter. Do not delete
or rewrite the original prose without logging the change. The corrections system
renders these automatically at the bottom of the article.

```yaml
corrections:
  - date: "2026-03-12"
    description: "An earlier version of this article stated the contract was awarded in March 2025. It was awarded in September 2024."
```

Include both what was wrong and what it now says. "Corrected a date" is not enough.

## Publishing Checklist

### Before submitting for editorial review

- [ ] All required frontmatter fields are complete
- [ ] `author:` slug matches an entry in `src/_data/authorProfiles/`
- [ ] `section:` is one of the 8 valid values (exact capitalization)
- [ ] `description:` is ≤180 characters and works without the headline
- [ ] `imageAlt:` is present if `image:` is set
- [ ] `status: draft` is set
- [ ] `draft: true` is set (keeps the piece off the live site until approved)
- [ ] All primary sources are documented (in the piece or in PR comments)
- [ ] Direct quotes have been confirmed against notes or recording
- [ ] Any corrections since the draft stage are logged in `corrections:`

### Before publishing (editor checklist)

- [ ] Factual claims spot-checked against sources
- [ ] Frontmatter complete and valid
- [ ] Author profile exists and is accurate
- [ ] `description:` works as a standalone summary
- [ ] Images have alt text; rights confirmed
- [ ] `status: published` set
- [ ] `draft: true` removed
- [ ] Series navigation correct if `series:` is set
- [ ] Future date set correctly if scheduling (article will publish at 00:05 UTC
  the day after the cron job runs — allow 24 hours)
- [ ] Social/newsletter notification planned
