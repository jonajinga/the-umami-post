prerequisites: A sharp paring or chef's knife, fresh basil (or mint, sage, sorrel — any soft, broad-leafed herb).
whyMatters: |
  <p>Chiffonade (French for "made of rags") is how you turn fresh basil into thin green ribbons that scatter over pasta or salad without bruising into the black-tinged mush you get from chopping. The technique is forty seconds of work and the difference between a dish that looks finished and one that looks slapped together.</p>
  <p>Two principles do all the work: <em>roll the leaves tightly</em> so the knife meets a small, uniform target, and <em>cut once with a clean stroke</em> instead of sawing. Sawing tears cell walls and oxidises the cut surfaces. A clean slice keeps the green green.</p>
instructions:
  - body: "<p>Wash the leaves and dry them <em>completely</em>. Wet leaves stick together when you roll them and bruise when the knife meets them. A salad spinner is the right tool; paper towels work too. Take an extra ten seconds to make them bone-dry.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Stack the leaves face-up, largest on the bottom, smallest on top. Five to eight leaves is the right batch size &mdash; more than that and the roll loosens.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Roll the stack from one long edge to the other, like a cigar. The roll should be tight but not crushed. If it cracks, ease up.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Place the roll seam-side down on the cutting board. Position your guiding hand in a claw grip, knuckles forward, fingertips tucked.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Slice across the roll with a single forward-pushing stroke per cut, about 2mm thick. The knife should make contact with the herb, glide forward through it, and finish on the board &mdash; not a sawing motion. If your knife is sharp, you should hear almost nothing.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Tease the ribbons apart with the tip of the knife. They should look like green silk thread. If they're matted, your knife wasn't sharp enough or the leaves were damp.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Use immediately. Chiffonade'd basil starts to oxidise the moment it's cut. If you must hold it for a few minutes, pile it loosely (don't compress) and cover with a damp paper towel.</p>"
commonMistakes: |
  <p><strong>Wet leaves.</strong> The single biggest cause of bruising. Water on the surface multiplies the friction between leaf and knife.</p>
  <p><strong>Dull knife.</strong> If you have to push hard, you're tearing instead of cutting. Sharpen the knife or use the smallest paring blade you own &mdash; a small sharp knife beats a big dull one.</p>
  <p><strong>Cutting too thick.</strong> 2-3mm is the sweet spot. Thicker and the ribbons read as chopped; thinner and they collapse into thread that disappears in the dish.</p>
  <p><strong>Sawing.</strong> The whole point is one clean stroke per slice. Sawing back and forth tears cells, which is the actual chemistry of bruised basil.</p>

The same two minutes invested before you plate dinner change a meal in a way that's hard to articulate but easy to see &mdash; the ribbons catch light, they sit on top of the food instead of falling between the cracks, and they taste freshly bright instead of bruised-vegetal. It is, objectively, the highest leverage-to-effort ratio in the home cook's toolkit.
