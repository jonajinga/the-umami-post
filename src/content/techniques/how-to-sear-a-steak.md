prerequisites: A 12-inch cast-iron skillet, a thick steak, and the ability to set off your smoke alarm.
whyMatters: |
  <p>Steak crust isn't about heat. It's about contact, dryness, and time. Most home cooks turn the burner on, panic at the smoke, and pull the steak before the Maillard reaction can do its full job. The result is grey-brown, not deep amber.</p>
  <p>This method is about controlling those three variables &mdash; making contact total, getting the surface bone-dry, and giving the crust the full ninety seconds it actually needs.</p>
instructions:
  - body: "<p>Pull the steak out of the fridge at least 45 minutes before cooking. A cold steak is a steamed-from-the-inside steak. Salt both sides aggressively with kosher salt and leave it on a wire rack &mdash; uncovered &mdash; on the counter. The salt pulls moisture out, then redissolves into the meat. This is the most important step. Skip it and the rest doesn't matter.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Pat the steak dry with paper towels right before cooking. Like, really dry. Any surface water becomes steam, and steam means no crust. If you can press the towel against the steak and it comes away damp, you aren't done patting.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Heat the cast-iron over medium-high for 4 to 5 full minutes. Add a thin film of neutral oil and let it shimmer &mdash; you should see ripple patterns moving across the surface. If the oil smokes immediately, the pan is too hot; pull it off the burner for 30 seconds.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Lay the steak away from you to avoid splatter. <em>Don't move it.</em> Set a timer for 90 seconds. Do not poke it, lift it, or peek under it. Use this minute and a half to open a window or turn on your hood &mdash; the smoke alarm is the price of a real crust.</p>"
  - body: "<p>At 90 seconds, flip the steak. Repeat: don't move it for another 90 seconds. The second side will brown faster than the first because the pan is even hotter now.</p>"
  - body: "<p>For a 1-inch ribeye, this two-minute total cook gets you medium-rare. For thicker cuts (1.5 inch or above), pull the steak after 60 seconds per side and finish in a 400&deg;F oven until the internal temperature is 125&deg;F (52&deg;C) for medium-rare. Use a thermometer; touch tests are unreliable and visual tests are lies.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Tip the steak onto a wire rack &mdash; not a plate &mdash; and rest at least 5 minutes. A plate traps steam underneath and softens the crust you just spent eight minutes building.</p>"
  - body: "<p>Slice against the grain. Salt the slices with flaky salt right before serving. Eat.</p>"
commonMistakes: |
  <p><strong>Cold steak from the fridge.</strong> A cold center forces you to cook longer, and the longer the steak is in contact with hot iron, the further past medium-rare you'll go before the crust is done.</p>
  <p><strong>Wet surface.</strong> The single biggest culprit for grey, sad crust. Pat with paper towels. Then pat again. Then pat once more after you've moved the steak to be sure.</p>
  <p><strong>Moving the steak.</strong> Every time you lift, slide, or flip the steak, you reset the crust clock. The Maillard reaction needs uninterrupted contact. Let it.</p>
  <p><strong>Resting on a plate.</strong> The steam has to go somewhere. On a plate, it softens the bottom crust into mush. A wire rack lets steam escape and the crust stays crisp.</p>

The five extra minutes of fridge-to-counter time, the obsessive towel-patting, the rest on a wire rack &mdash; none of these feel like cooking. They feel like preparation. And that's the trick: a good steak is 90% preparation and 10% standing absolutely still over a hot pan.
